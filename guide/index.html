📐 High anion gap metabolic acidosis (requires 1 finding):
AG = Na – (Cl + HCO₃) >12 mEq/L + 1 of: DKA, lactic acidosis, uremia (GFR<30), methanol/ethylene glycol, salicylates, pyroglutamic acidosis, or starvation ketosis (severe).
🩸 Delta Ratio (ΔAG/ΔHCO₃) for mixed disorders:
• <0.4 → non-AG acidosis (hyperchloremic) co-existing
• 0.4–0.8 → normal AG + high AG mixed (e.g., DKA with normal saline)
• 1.0–2.0 → pure high AG acidosis (DKA, lactic acidosis)
• >2.0 → concurrent metabolic alkalosis (vomiting, diuretics)

• HbA1c 5.7–6.4% (39–47 mmol/mol)

• Fasting ≥92 mg/dL (5.1 mmol/L)

• 2h ≥153 mg/dL (8.5 mmol/L)

• HOMA-IR (fasting insulin × fasting glucose / 405) → >2.5 suggests insulin resistance.
• Triglyceride/HDL ratio >3.0 (mg/dL) or >1.3 (mmol/L).

• P/F ratio (PaO₂ / FiO₂): Normal >400; <300 defines acute hypoxemic respiratory failure.

🔴 ARDS (Berlin Definition 2012) – requires ALL 4 criteria:

② Chest imaging (CXR or CT): Bilateral opacities – not fully explained by effusions, lobar/lung collapse, or nodules.
③ Origin of edema: Respiratory failure not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload. Need objective assessment (e.g., echocardiography) to exclude hydrostatic edema if no risk factor present.

🩸 Hypoxemia severity classification (non‑ARDS context):
• Mild hypoxemia: PaO₂ 60–80 mmHg (SpO₂ 90–94%)
• Moderate hypoxemia: PaO₂ 45–59 mmHg (SpO₂ 75–89%)
• Severe hypoxemia: PaO₂ <45 mmHg (SpO₂ <75%)
• Refractory hypoxemia: failure to maintain SpO₂ >88% despite FiO₂ ≥0.8 + PEEP ≥10
🩺 Indications for high-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) or NIV in acute respiratory failure (requires ≥1):
• RR >24 breaths/min
• PaCO₂ >45 mmHg with respiratory acidosis (pH 7.25–7.35)
• SpO₂ <90% on FiO₂ ≥0.4
• Accessory muscle use or paradoxical abdominal breathing
📈 ROX index (SpO₂/FiO₂ ÷ RR) to predict HFNC success: ≥4.88 at 2h, 6h, 12h predicts low intubation risk. ROX <3.85 at 12h → high failure, consider intubation.

🧬 CKD classification (KDIGO 2024 updated)

≥3 months duration + 1 of 3 abnormalities
📌 CKD diagnosis requires (duration ≥3 months) ANY 1 of the following:
• eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m² (G3a–G5)
• Albuminuria (ACR ≥30 mg/g = A2 or A3)
• Persistent abnormalities in urine sediment (hematuria, casts)
• Structural abnormalities on imaging (polycystic kidneys, hydronephrosis, small kidneys)
• Histologic evidence of kidney damage (biopsy)
• Electrolyte/acid-base disorders due to tubular dysfunction (history of kidney transplantation also qualifies)

• Green (low risk): G1–G2 + A1 → annual eGFR decline ~0.5–1 mL/min, low CV risk

📈 CKD progression risk categories (KDIGO 2024, based on 2-year risk of ESKD):
• Low risk: eGFR ≥60 + ACR <30 → annual eGFR loss <1 mL/min
• Moderate risk: eGFR 45–59 + ACR 30–300 → annual eGFR loss 1–2 mL/min
• High risk: eGFR 30–44 + ACR 30–300 OR eGFR ≥60 + ACR >300 → annual loss 2–4 mL/min
• Very high risk: eGFR <30 + any ACR OR eGFR 30–44 + ACR >300 → annual loss >4 mL/min, ESKD within 5–10 years
🧫 Albuminuria interpretation (requires 2 of 3 positive samples within 3–6 months):
• Transient albuminuria: fever, exercise, UTI, heart failure, hyperglycemia → resolves after treatment
• Persistent albuminuria: diabetic kidney disease, hypertensive nephrosclerosis, primary glomerulonephritis
• Nephrotic-range proteinuria: UACR >2200 mg/g (or urine protein >3.5 g/24h) → suggests glomerular disease (e.g., FSGS, membranous, diabetic nephropathy with advanced lesions)
🧪 Additional CKD biomarkers and parameters:
• Cystatin C-based eGFR (eGFRcys): recommended when muscle mass abnormal (amputation, sarcopenia, bodybuilders); eGFRcr-cys more accurate than creatinine alone.
• Urea-to-creatinine ratio (BUN:Cr): >20 suggests prerenal state or GI bleed; <10 suggests liver disease or low protein intake.
• Fractional excretion of sodium (FeNa): <1% suggests prerenal AKI in oliguric patients; >2% suggests intrinsic renal injury.
• Urine sediment: acanthocytes (glomerular bleeding), muddy brown casts (ATN), WBC casts (interstitial nephritis), RBC casts (glomerulonephritis)
🩺 Nephrology referral triggers (≥1 of the following):
• eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m² (G4–G5)
• Persistent ACR >300 mg/g (A3)
• Rapid eGFR decline (≥25% drop in 1 year OR >5 mL/min/year loss in 2 consecutive years)
• Hyperkalemia (K⁺ >5.5 mmol/L) refractory to medical management
• Uncontrolled hypertension despite ≥3 agents (including diuretic)
• Suspected non-diabetic kidney disease (hematuria, dysmorphic RBCs, rapid onset proteinuria, active sediment)
• Anemia of CKD (Hb <10 g/dL) requiring evaluation for ESA
• Mineral bone disorder (Ca, P, PTH abnormalities) in CKD G3b–G5
🧮 eGFR estimating equations (clinical use):
• CKD-EPI 2021 (creatinine): preferred, no race coefficient
• CKD-EPI creatinine-cystatin C: more accurate, recommended when more precision needed (e.g., drug dosing, kidney donor evaluation)
• MDRD Study equation: older, less accurate at GFR >60
• Bedside Schwartz (pediatric): for children age 1–16 years
⚠️ CKD-related complications by stage (monitoring checklist):
• G1–G2 (early): BP control, ACEi/ARB if albuminuric, annual ACR/eGFR
• G3a (moderate): Anemia screen (CBC, ferritin), bone profile (Ca, P, PTH), metabolic acidosis (HCO₃)
• G3b–G4 (advanced): Hyperkalemia monitoring, phosphate binders if hyperphosphatemia, consider bicarbonate for HCO₃ <22, CKD-MBD management
• G5 (kidney failure): Prepare for RRT (fistula/peritoneal dialysis catheter), discuss transplantation, manage uremia symptoms

🔬 Morphologic classification (based on MCV) – requires 1 of 3:
• Microcytic anemia (MCV <80 fL): iron deficiency, thalassemia, anemia of chronic disease (some), sideroblastic anemia

🧬 Reticulocyte production index (RPI) – distinguishes hypoproliferative vs hyperproliferative anemia:

• RPI <2 → hypoproliferative (iron deficiency, ACD, bone marrow failure)
• RPI >2–3 → appropriate response to anemia (hemolysis or blood loss)
• RPI >3 → excessive inappropriate response (rare)

🧪 Pernicious anemia (autoimmune metaplastic atrophic gastritis) – specific criteria (≥1 of):
• Anti-intrinsic factor antibody (IFAb): 50–70% sensitivity, >95% specificity
• Anti-parietal cell antibody (PCA): 80–90% sensitivity, lower specificity (also positive in Hashimoto, vitiligo)
• Abnormal Schilling test (historical, largely replaced by serology + endoscopic biopsy)
• Gastric biopsy: severe corpus-restricted atrophy, enterochromaffin-like cell hyperplasia
⚠️ B12 deficiency complications – neurologic and hematologic:
• Neurologic: subacute combined degeneration (posterior column loss – impaired vibration/proprioception, sensory ataxia), peripheral neuropathy, cognitive impairment, optic atrophy
• Hematologic: macrocytic anemia (MCV >100), hypersegmented neutrophils (>5 lobes), pancytopenia (severe cases)
• Distinct from folate deficiency: folate deficiency causes same hematologic changes but NO neurologic damage; if neurologic signs present → B12 deficiency until proven otherwise
💉 Treatment protocols:
• B12 deficiency (severe/neurologic): 1000 μg IM daily x1 week, then weekly x4 weeks, then monthly for life (if malabsorption/pernicious anemia)
• Mild deficiency without neurologic signs: oral B12 1000–2000 μg daily effective in many (except pernicious anemia or severe malabsorption)
• Folate deficiency: folic acid 1–5 mg daily; IMPORTANT: never give folate without ruling out B12 deficiency (can mask hematologic signs while neurologic damage progresses)

🧬 Beta-thalassemia spectrum: major, intermedia, minor (trait)

major: 5/5; intermedia: variable; trait: ≥3/6
🧫 Beta-thalassemia major (Cooley anemia) – requires ALL 5:
① Age at presentation: 6–24 months (severe anemia after fall of HbF)
② Hemoglobin (untreated): <7 g/dL (often 3–6 g/dL)
③ MCV: <70 fL (often 50–65 fL)
④ Hemoglobin electrophoresis: HbF >60% (often 60–98%), HbA2 variable (usually normal or low, not elevated as in trait), HbA absent or minimal
⑤ Transfusion dependence: lifelong, starting before age 2 years
🩸 Beta-thalassemia intermedia (non-transfusion dependent but clinically significant) – ≥3 of:
• Age >2 years at presentation
• Hb 7–10 g/dL (maintained without regular transfusions)
• HbF 10–50% (variable)
• Clinical sequelae: extramedullary hematopoiesis, leg ulcers, gallstones, osteopenia, pulmonary hypertension
• Genetic confirmation: β⁺/β⁰ compound or homozygous β⁺ with modifying factors (α-thal co-inheritance, HPFH)
🔬 Beta-thalassemia minor (trait) – requires ≥3 of following 6:
① Mild microcytic anemia: Hb 9–12 g/dL (often 10–12)
② MCV <75 fL (often 60–75 fL)
③ RBC count elevated: >5.0 × 10¹²/L (erythrocytosis)
④ HbA2 elevated: >3.5% (typically 4–8%) – hallmark diagnostic feature
⑤ Mentzer index (MCV/RBC) <13 (typical 10–12)
⑥ Family history: parent with beta-thalassemia trait

💓 Right ventricular hypertrophy (RVH) – ECG criteria

requires ANY 2 of 10 (major/minor criteria)
Major criteria (high specificity):
• R in V1 >7 mm (0.7 mV)
• R/S ratio in V1 >1 (R wave taller than S)
• qR complex in V1 (absence of septal q wave in left precordium)
• Right axis deviation >+110° (or >+100° in some guidelines)
• R in V1 + S in V5 or V6 >10.5 mm (1.05 mV)
Minor criteria (supportive):
• S in V5 or V6 >7 mm (0.7 mV)
• R/S ratio in V5 or V6 <1
• T wave inversion in V1–V3 (right precordial strain pattern)
• P pulmonale (RAE) – P wave amplitude >2.5 mm in II, III, aVF
• Incomplete or complete RBBB pattern with right axis deviation
📐 Additional RVH indices (for challenging cases):
• Butler-Leggett criteria: R in V1 + S in V5/V6 >10.5 mm AND right axis deviation >+90° (sensitivity ~60%, specificity >90%)
• Morris index: P terminal force in V1 >0.04 mm·s (suggests right atrial abnormality)
• EKG in chronic cor pulmonale: RVH + RAE + right axis deviation
🫁 Causes of RVH on ECG (clinical correlation required):
• Pulmonary hypertension (any cause: PAH, CTEPH, left heart disease, lung disease)
• Chronic lung disease (COPD, interstitial lung disease, OSA)
• Congenital heart disease (Tetralogy of Fallot, VSD, ASD, Ebstein anomaly)
• Pulmonic stenosis (valvular or infundibular)
• Mitral stenosis (secondary pulmonary hypertension)
• Acute cor pulmonale (massive PE) – may show acute RBBB, S1Q3T3

⚡ Bundle branch blocks & intraventricular conduction delays

LBBB: 5/5; RBBB: 4/4; plus incomplete variants
Complete Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB) – requires ALL 5:
① QRS duration ≥120 ms (adults), ≥100 ms (children 4–16 years)
② Broad, notched or slurred R wave in leads I, aVL, V5, V6 (lateral leads)
③ Absent Q waves in lateral leads (I, aVL, V5, V6) – no septal q waves
④ RS or QS complex in leads V1, V2 (deep S waves, small or absent R)
⑤ Intrinsicoid deflection (R wave peak time) in V5/V6 >60 ms (0.06 sec)
Complete Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB) – requires ALL 4:
① QRS duration ≥120 ms (adults), ≥100 ms (children)
② rsR', rSR', or qR complex in lead V1 (rabbit ear or M-shaped QRS)
③ Wide, slurred S wave in leads I, aVL, V5, V6 (duration greater than R wave)
④ Discordant ST-T changes: ST depression and T wave inversion in right precordial leads (V1–V3)
Incomplete bundle branch blocks:
• Incomplete LBBB: QRS duration 100–119 ms, LBBB morphology present (lateral notching, absent septal Q, V1/V2 QS pattern)
• Incomplete RBBB: QRS duration 100–119 ms, rsr' or rSr' in V1 (normal S in V5/V6 may be preserved)
• Nonspecific intraventricular conduction delay (IVCD): QRS ≥110 ms without criteria for LBBB or RBBB
🚨 LBBB in acute myocardial infarction (Sgarbossa criteria – modified 2021):
Score ≥3 points = high specificity for acute MI in LBBB:
• Discordant ST elevation ≥1 mm and concordant with QRS (Sgarbossa sign 3A): 5 points
• Concordant ST elevation ≥1 mm in any lead (Sgarbossa sign 1): 5 points
• Concordant ST depression ≥1 mm in V1–V3 (Sgarbossa sign 2): 3 points
• Discordant ST elevation ≥1 mm with proportionally excessive (≥25% of S wave depth): 2 points (modified criteria)
🔄 Rate-dependent (phase 3) bundle branch block:
• Appears at heart rates above a critical threshold (tachycardia-dependent)
• Resolves when heart rate slows – suggests underlying diseased conducting system

📈 Atrial enlargement (LAE / RAE / Bi-atrial abnormality)

requires 1/3 criteria for each atrium
Left Atrial Enlargement (LAE) – P mitrale – requires ANY 1 of:
① P wave duration >120 ms (0.12 sec) in lead II (broad P wave)
② Notched P wave in lead II (P mitrale) with interpeak distance >40 ms (0.04 sec)
③ P terminal force (PTF) in V1: negative deflection depth (mm) × duration (sec) >0.04 mm·s (normal <0.02)
④ P wave axis more leftward (P wave upright in I, positive/negative in II, III, aVF variable)
Right Atrial Enlargement (RAE) – P pulmonale – requires ANY 1 of:
① P wave amplitude >2.5 mm (0.25 mV) in leads II, III, aVF (peaked/tall P wave)
② P wave axis >+75° (rightward shift)
③ Initial positive deflection in V1 or V2 >1.5 mm (0.15 mV) (peaked upright P in right precordium)
④ P wave amplitude >1.5 mm in V1–V2 with duration <120 ms
Bi-atrial enlargement (P mitrale + P pulmonale simultaneously):
• Criteria for LAE + RAE both met on same ECG
• Often seen in advanced heart failure, mixed valvular disease (mitral stenosis + tricuspid regurgitation), restrictive/hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
📐 P wave axis calculation and interpretation:
• Normal P axis: 0° to +75° (P upright in I, II, aVF; P may be biphasic in III)
• Leftward P axis (<0°): LAE, inferior MI, ectopic atrial rhythm
• Rightward P axis (>+75°): RAE, dextrocardia, limb lead reversal, COPD
• Vertical P axis (+75° to +90°): thin asthenic habitus, COPD

🫀 Pulmonary embolism – ECG manifestations & scoring

requires ≥2 findings (Daniel score ≥1 suggests PE)
Classic ECG findings in acute pulmonary embolism (frequency varies):
• Sinus tachycardia (>100 bpm) – most common (40–70%)
• S1Q3T3 pattern (S wave in I, Q wave in III, T inversion in III) – classic but low sensitivity (~20%)
• T wave inversions in right precordial leads V1–V4 (specific, seen in ~30–40%)
• New-onset incomplete or complete RBBB (transient)
• Right axis deviation (>+90° or new)

🩺 Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) – Rotterdam 2003 / AE-PCOS 2018

requires 2 of 3 (Rotterdam) after exclusion
Rotterdam criteria (2003, most widely used) – requires 2 of 3 + exclusion of other causes:
① Oligo-ovulation or anovulation: menstrual cycles >35 days or <8 cycles/year (oligomenorrhea) OR amenorrhea ≥3 months
② Clinical and/or biochemical hyperandrogenism:
- Clinical: hirsutism (modified Ferriman-Gallwey score ≥8), severe acne, alopecia, acanthosis nigricans
- Biochemical: total testosterone >50 ng/dL (1.7 nmol/L), free testosterone elevated, DHEA-S >350 μg/dL
③ Polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound: ≥20 follicles (2–9 mm) per ovary OR ovarian volume ≥10 mL (no dominant follicle or corpus luteum)
Exclusion: thyroid dysfunction, hyperprolactinemia, non-classic congenital adrenal hyperplasia (17-OHP), androgen-secreting tumor, Cushing syndrome
PCOS phenotypes (4 subtypes – important for management):
• Phenotype A (full, classic): hyperandrogenism + ovulatory dysfunction + PCOM → most severe metabolic risk
• Phenotype B (non-PCOM): hyperandrogenism + ovulatory dysfunction (without PCOM)
• Phenotype C (ovulatory): hyperandrogenism + PCOM (regular cycles) → milder
• Phenotype D (non-hyperandrogenic): ovulatory dysfunction + PCOM (no hyperandrogenism)
🩸 Androgen excess society (AE-PCOS 2006/2018) criteria – stricter, requires ALL 3:
• Hyperandrogenism (clinical or biochemical)
• Ovarian dysfunction (oligo-ovulation or PCOM)
• Exclusion of other androgen excess disorders
🧪 Laboratory evaluation in suspected PCOS (recommended panel):
• Total testosterone, free testosterone (calculated or equilibrium dialysis)
• DHEA-S (adrenal source)
• 17-hydroxyprogesterone (to exclude 21-hydroxylase deficiency – CVAH)
• Prolactin, TSH, FSH, LH (LH:FSH ratio >2:1 suggestive)
• AMH (Anti-Müllerian hormone) – often elevated in PCOS (>4 ng/mL)
• Fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel

🧬 Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN1, MEN2A, MEN2B, MEN4) – genetic & clinical criteria

clinical diagnosis: ≥2 MEN-associated tumors OR 1 tumor + family history
MEN1 (Wermer syndrome) – MEN1 gene mutation (11q13) – requires ≥2 of 3 major tumors:
• Parathyroid adenoma/hyperplasia (>95% penetrance by age 50) – hypercalcemia, elevated PTH
• Pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor (pNET) – gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison), insulinoma, glucagonoma, VIPoma (30–80%)
• Pituitary adenoma (30–40%) – prolactinoma most common, then non-functioning, GH, ACTH
• Supporting tumors: adrenal cortical tumors, facial angiofibromas, collagenomas, lipomas, meningiomas, thymic/bronchial carcinoids
MEN2A (Sipple syndrome) – RET proto-oncogene mutation (10q11.21):
• Medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) – 100% penetrance by age 30 (earliest manifestation)
• Pheochromocytoma (50% penetrance) – often bilateral, medullary predominant
• Parathyroid hyperplasia/adenoma (20–30%) – mild hypercalcemia
• Variants: Familial MTC (FMTC) – MTC only without pheochromocytoma or hyperparathyroidism
MEN2B (most aggressive) – RET M918T mutation (95%):
• Medullary thyroid carcinoma (very early, by age 1–5 years, highly aggressive)
• Pheochromocytoma (50%, often bilateral)
• Distinctive phenotype: Mucosal neuromas (lips, tongue), Marfanoid habitus (tall, slender, pectus, joint laxity), medullated corneal nerve fibers, GI ganglioneuromatosis (chronic constipation, megacolon)
MEN4 (CDKN1B mutation – rare):
• Parathyroid adenoma/hyperplasia (most common)
• Pituitary adenoma, pNETs, adrenocortical tumors, renal angiomyolipoma, testicular cancer
• Phenotype overlaps with MEN1 but MEN1 gene mutation negative
🧬 Genetic testing indications for MEN syndromes (≥1 of):
• Medullary thyroid carcinoma (any age, sporadic or familial) → RET testing
• Pheochromocytoma (especially bilateral or familial) → RET, VHL, SDHx, NF1
• MEN1-associated tumor in patient <35 years
• First-degree relative of known MEN mutation carrier
• Hyperparathyroidism + pNET or pituitary tumor in same patient

💧 Pulmonary Edema – Cardiogenic (Hemodynamic) vs Non-Cardiogenic (ARDS/Neurogenic)

clinical + radiographic + hemodynamic criteria
Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema (Hydrostatic Pulmonary Edema) – requires ≥3 of:
① Symptoms: dyspnea, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, pink frothy sputum
② Signs: rales/crackles (bilateral, basal), S3 gallop, JVD, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly, hepatojugular reflux
③ CXR findings: cardiomegaly, Kerley B lines, perihilar batwing opacities, pleural effusions, cephalization (upper lobe redistribution)
④ Hemodynamics: PAWP (pulmonary artery wedge pressure) >18 mmHg (invasive gold standard) OR LVEDP >15 mmHg
⑤ Echo: LVEF reduced (HFrEF) or preserved with elevated filling pressures (HFpEF: E/e' >15, TR velocity >2.8 m/s)
⑥ BNP/NT-proBNP: BNP >400 pg/mL, NT-proBNP >1200 pg/mL (higher if age >75, renal dysfunction)
Non-Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema (ARDS, permeability edema) – requires ALL 4 (Berlin) AND PAWP ≤18 mmHg:
• Acute onset within 1 week of insult
• Bilateral opacities on CXR/CT (not fully explained by effusion or collapse)
• Not fully explained by cardiac failure (PAWP ≤18 if measured, or no evidence of LA hypertension on echo)
• P/F ratio ≤300 with PEEP ≥5 cmH₂O
Causes: Sepsis, aspiration, pancreatitis, trauma, transfusion-related (TRALI), inhalational, drug-induced
Neurogenic Pulmonary Edema (NPE) – associated with CNS injury, requires ALL:
① Acute CNS insult (SAH, TBI, ICH, seizure, stroke, spinal cord injury)
② Acute respiratory distress within minutes to hours of CNS injury
③ CXR: bilateral symmetric infiltrates (similar to cardiogenic but PAWP normal)
④ Hemodynamics: normal cardiac function, normal PAWP (<15 mmHg)
⑤ Resolution: usually rapid (24-72 hours) with CNS recovery

🦋 Hyperthyroidism (Thyrotoxicosis) – Graves, Toxic Nodule, Thyroiditis

low TSH + elevated FT4/T3 + etiology-specific criteria
Biochemical Diagnosis (requires ALL for overt hyperthyroidism):
① TSH suppressed (<0.4 mIU/L, often undetectable <0.01 mIU/L)
② Free T4 (FT4) elevated (>1.8 ng/dL or >23 pmol/L)
③ Total T3 or Free T3 elevated (T3 toxicosis: normal FT4, low TSH, elevated T3)
Subclinical hyperthyroidism: TSH <0.4 mIU/L but normal FT4 and FT3 (repeat in 2–4 weeks)
Graves' Disease (most common cause) – requires ≥2 of:
• Diffuse goiter (smooth, symmetric, ± bruit)
• Graves' ophthalmopathy (proptosis, lid lag, periorbital edema, diplopia, chemosis)
• TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) positive (>95% sensitivity, >99% specificity)
• Thyroid stimulating immunoglobulin (TSI) positive
• Thyroid scan (Tc99m or I123): diffuse uptake (>20% at 24h)
Toxic Adenoma (Autonomous Functioning Thyroid Nodule) – requires:
• Solitary (or multiple) hyperfunctioning nodule on thyroid scan (hot nodule, suppresses remainder of gland)
• Thyroid ultrasound confirms nodule (typically >2–3 cm)
• TRAb negative
• Toxic multinodular goiter (Plummer disease): multiple hyperfunctioning nodules on scan, usually older patients
Thyroiditis (Subacute/De Quervain, Painless/Silent, Postpartum) – features:
• Triphasic thyroiditis: thyrotoxicosis → hypothyroidism → recovery (weeks to months)
• Low radioactive iodine uptake (RAIU) on scan (<5% at 24h) – distinguishes from Graves (which has high uptake)
• Subacute: painful goiter, fever, ESR/CRP elevated, viral prodrome
• Painless/silent: autoimmune (postpartum variant), lymphocytic infiltration, no pain, may progress to hypothyroidism

🦴 Parathyroid disorders – Primary Hyperparathyroidism (PHPT) & Hypoparathyroidism

elevated or inappropriately normal PTH with hypercalcemia (PHPT); low PTH with hypocalcemia
Primary Hyperparathyroidism (PHPT) – requires ALL (after excluding secondary causes):
① Hypercalcemia: total calcium >10.2 mg/dL (2.55 mmol/L) – confirmed on at least 2 occasions
② PTH (intact) elevated OR inappropriately normal for calcium level (PTH >20 pg/mL in presence of hypercalcemia)
③ Normal or high 25-hydroxyvitamin D (vitamin D deficiency excluded; replete before diagnosis)
④ Renal function normal or stable (exclude secondary hyperparathyroidism from CKD)
Normocalcemic PHPT (controversial): normal total calcium, elevated PTH, normal vitamin D, normal renal function, exclude secondary causes
Surgical Criteria for Parathyroidectomy (≥1 of following – 4th International Workshop):
• Age <50 years
• Serum calcium >1 mg/dL above upper limit of normal (>11.2 mg/dL)
• eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m² (renal impairment)
• 24h urinary calcium >400 mg/day (nephrolithiasis risk)
• Kidney stone on imaging (any – even asymptomatic)
• Osteoporosis (T-score ≤ -2.5 at any site) OR fragility fracture
• Vertebral fracture (on imaging, any)
• Significant neurocognitive symptoms or myopathy interfering with daily activities
Imaging for PHPT localization (preoperative):
• Technetium-99m sestamibi scan (SPECT/CT): first-line, sensitivity 80-90% for single adenoma
• Neck ultrasound: localization, identifies thyroid nodules (concomitant disease)
• 4D-CT: high resolution, especially for ectopic glands (mediastinum, intrathyroidal)
• Selective venous sampling: for recurrent or persistent disease after prior surgery
Hypoparathyroidism (requires ALL):
① Hypocalcemia: corrected total calcium <8.5 mg/dL (2.12 mmol/L) OR ionized calcium <1.12 mmol/L
② PTH low or inappropriately normal (PTH <20 pg/mL in setting of hypocalcemia)
③ Exclusion of other causes of hypocalcemia (low magnesium, vitamin D deficiency, CKD, PTH resistance)
Etiologies: Post-surgical (thyroid, parathyroid, radical neck dissection) – most common; Autoimmune (isolated or APS1); Genetic (DiGeorge, CASR mutation, Barakat syndrome); Magnesium deficiency (functional hypoparathyroidism); Infiltrative (hemochromatosis, Wilson, sarcoid)
🔬 Complications and monitoring of hypoparathyroidism:
• Acute: tetany (Chvostek sign, Trousseau sign), QT prolongation, torsade, laryngospasm, seizures
• Chronic: basal ganglia calcifications, cataracts (posterior subcapsular), nephrocalcinosis (with high calcium-phosphorus product), bone disease
• Treatment: calcium (elemental 1-2g/day) + calcitriol (0.25-2 mcg/day) – PTH replacement (rhPTH 1-84) for refractory cases
Secondary and Tertiary Hyperparathyroidism – diagnostic differentiation:
• Secondary HPT: Chronic kidney disease (eGFR <45) → hypocalcemia, hyperphosphatemia → PTH elevated. Managed medically (calcitriol, phosphate binders, cinacalcet).
• Tertiary HPT: Long-term dialysis, after kidney transplant → autonomous PTH secretion (hypercalcemia + elevated PTH). Indication for parathyroidectomy if severe.

🫀 Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) – UA, NSTEMI, STEMI Diagnostic Criteria

ECG changes + troponin rise/fall + symptoms – classification by ECG and biomarkers
Unstable Angina (UA) – requires ALL of:
① No elevation of hs-cTn (<99th percentile URL) – normal serial troponins
② ECG: transient ST depression (≥0.5 mm), T-wave inversion (≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads), or non-diagnostic changes – may be normal
③ Symptoms: angina at rest (prolonged >20 min), new-onset severe angina (CCS III or higher), or accelerating angina (increased frequency/intensity)
④ No evidence of myocardial necrosis (CK-MB or troponin negative)
(Patients with UA and unstable pattern requiring urgent revascularization are high risk)
NSTEMI (Non-ST Elevation Myocardial Infarction) – requires BOTH:
① Rise and/or fall of hs-cTn with ≥1 value >99th percentile URL (dynamic pattern)
② No persistent ST-segment elevation on ECG (may have ST depression ≥0.5 mm, T wave inversion, or non-diagnostic ECG)
Additional supportive criteria: ischemic symptoms (>20 min rest angina), ECG changes, imaging evidence of new loss of viable myocardium, angiographic evidence of thrombus

🫀 Angina Pectoris – Stable Angina (CCS Classification) & Variant (Prinzmetal) Angina

CCS functional class I–IV + provocative testing or angiographic confirmation
Canadian Cardiovascular Society (CCS) Angina Grading (stable exertional angina):
• Class I: Ordinary physical activity (walking, climbing stairs) does NOT cause angina – occurs only with strenuous, rapid, or prolonged exertion
• Class II: Slight limitation – angina with walking >2 blocks or climbing >1 flight of stairs at normal pace, or with emotional stress
• Class III: Marked limitation – angina with walking 1-2 blocks or climbing 1 flight of stairs at normal pace
• Class IV: Inability to perform any physical activity without discomfort – angina may occur at rest
Variant (Prinzmetal) Angina (Coronary Vasospasm) – requires ≥2 of:
① Angina at rest (typically between midnight and early morning) – transient, often severe
② Transient ST-segment elevation (≥1 mm) during symptoms, normalizing with resolution
③ Provocative testing: coronary angiography with ergonovine or acetylcholine – focal or diffuse vasospasm (occlusive/subocclusive)
④ Absence of significant fixed coronary stenosis (>50%) on baseline angiography (but may co-exist with non-obstructive plaque)
⑤ Response to calcium channel blockers (high-dose diltiazem, nifedipine, verapamil)

• Inferior vena cava (IVC) syndrome / Pelvic mass compressing iliac veins: bilateral leg edema, pelvic or abdominal mass, associated with malignancy (ovarian, uterine, bladder, prostate), imaging required

🦴 Osteoarthritis (OA) – Clinical & Radiographic Criteria (ACR 1990/2019)

ACR criteria for knee OA: requires knee pain + ≥3 of 6 features
ACR Clinical + Radiographic Criteria for Knee OA (requires knee pain + at least 3 of 6):
① Age >50 years
② Morning stiffness <30 minutes duration
③ Crepitus on active motion
④ Bony tenderness of knee
⑤ Bony enlargement on exam
⑥ No palpable warmth of synovium
Radiographic findings (Kellgren-Lawrence grading – required for severity):
Kellgren-Lawrence (KL) Grading Scale (0-4) for OA severity:
• Grade 0: Normal – no features of OA
• Grade 1: Doubtful – minute osteophyte, doubtful narrowing
• Grade 2: Minimal – definite osteophyte, possible joint space narrowing

🦶 Gout – ACR/EULAR 2015 Classification Criteria (point-based)

requires ≥8 points (out of 23) from clinical, lab, imaging domains
ACR/EULAR 2015 Gout Classification Criteria (≥8 points – sufficient for diagnosis without aspiration):
Domain 1: Pattern of joint/bursa involvement (max 4 pts):
• MTP1 involvement (2 pts) OR ankle/midfoot (1 pt)
• Typical flare pattern: redness, swelling, pain reaching maximum within 24h (2 pts)
Domain 2: Lab findings (max 8 pts):
• Serum urate (off urate-lowering therapy): >10 mg/dL (4 pts), >8–10 mg/dL (2 pts), 6–8 mg/dL (1 pt)
• Synovial fluid analysis (if obtained): MSU crystals present (4 pts) – gold standard
• Urate-lowering therapy not started at time of assessment
Domain 3: Imaging (max 12 pts):
• Ultrasound: Double-contour sign (4 pts) – crystal deposition on cartilage
• Dual-energy CT (DECT): Urate deposition (4 pts)
• Radiography: Erosion (4 pts)

Typical acute gout attack features (supports diagnosis):
• Sudden onset (often nocturnal, peak within 12-24h)
• Monoarticular (60-70% first MTP – podagra)
• Dramatic erythema, swelling, warmth, exquisite tenderness
• Self-limiting over 7-14 days if untreated
• Precipitants: alcohol, red meat, seafood, dehydration, thiazides, surgery, trauma

🖐️ Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) – ACR/EULAR 2010 Classification Criteria (point-based)

requires ≥6 points from 4 domains (confirmed synovitis of ≥1 joint, no alternate diagnosis)
Domain A: Joint involvement (score 0-5 pts):
• 1 large joint (0 pts) • 2-10 large joints (1 pt) • 1-3 small joints (2 pts) • 4-10 small joints (3 pts) • >10 joints (≥1 small joint) (5 pts)
(Large joints: shoulders, elbows, hips, knees, ankles. Small joints: MCPs, PIPs, MTPs, wrists)
Domain B: Serology (score 0-3 pts):
• Negative RF and negative anti-CCP (0 pts)
• Low-positive RF or low-positive anti-CCP (≤3× ULN) (2 pts)
• High-positive RF or high-positive anti-CCP (>3× ULN) (3 pts)
Domain C: Acute phase reactants (score 0-1 pt):
• Normal CRP and normal ESR (0 pts)
• Abnormal CRP OR abnormal ESR (1 pt)
Domain D: Symptom duration (score 0-1 pt):
• <6 weeks (0 pts) • ≥6 weeks (1 pt)
Imaging findings (not in score, but supportive):
• Ultrasound: synovial hypertrophy, power Doppler signal (active inflammation), tenosynovitis
• MRI: bone marrow edema (osteitis), synovitis, erosions
• Radiography (late): periarticular osteopenia, joint space narrowing, marginal erosions, subluxation (swan-neck, boutonnière, ulnar deviation)

requires radiographic sacroiliitis (grade ≥2 bilateral or grade 3-4 unilateral) + ≥1 clinical criterion

Radiographic sacroiliitis (required):
• Grade 0: Normal
• Grade 1: Suspicious
• Grade 2: Minimal sclerosis/erosion
• Grade 3: Moderate/marked sclerosis, erosions, possible ankylosis
• Grade 4: Complete ankylosis (bamboo spine)

① Low back pain and stiffness >3 months, improving with exercise, not relieved by rest

🦠 Reactive Arthritis (Reiter's Syndrome) – Diagnostic Criteria

requires arthritis + preceding infection (genitourinary or enteric) within 1-4 weeks + exclusion of other arthritis
Classic triad (complete Reiter's – occurs in <30%):
① Arthritis (asymmetric, oligoarticular, lower limb predominant – knees, ankles, feet)
② Conjunctivitis (often bilateral, mild, non-purulent, 30-50%)
③ Urethritis (dysuria, mucopurulent discharge, sterile pyuria) OR cervicitis in females
Additional clinical features (supportive, ≥1 of):
• Enthesitis (Achilles tendinitis, plantar fasciitis)
• Dactylitis (sausage digit)
• Keratoderma blennorrhagicum (hyperkeratotic papules/plaques on palms/soles)
• Circinate balanitis (painless, shallow ulcers on glans)
• Oral ulcers (painless, shallow, transient)
• Nail changes (onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis)
• Sacroiliitis (axial involvement)
Preceding infections (documentation helpful but not mandatory):
• Genitourinary: Chlamydia trachomatis, Ureaplasma urealyticum (symptoms 1-4 weeks after)
• Enteric: Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia (symptoms 1-4 weeks after)
• Laboratory: HLA-B27 positive (60-80%, especially with axial disease or severe presentation)

🖐️ Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA) – CASPAR Criteria (2006)

requires ≥3 points from 5 domains (must have inflammatory arthritis, psoriasis, or personal/family history)
CASPAR Criteria (ClASsification criteria for Psoriatic ARthritis) – points:
• Current psoriasis (2 pts) – or personal history of psoriasis (1 pt) – or family history of psoriasis (1 pt if patient not affected)
• Psoriatic nail dystrophy (1 pt): pitting, onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis, oil spots
• Negative rheumatoid factor (1 pt): any method except latex
• Dactylitis (1 pt): current or history (sausage digit)
• Juxta-articular new bone formation on X-ray (1 pt): ill-defined ossification near joint margins (hands, feet)
Clinical phenotypes of PsA (5 subtypes, may overlap):
• Symmetric polyarthritis (50-60%): resembles RA but RF negative, more DIP involvement
• Asymmetric oligoarthritis (30%): <5 joints, often involves DIPs
• Distal interphalangeal (DIP) predominant (5-10%): classic PsA pattern
• Axial/spondylitis (5-10%): sacroiliitis, syndesmophytes (asymmetric, chunky)
• Arthritis mutilans (<5%): severe, destructive, telescoping digits, pencil-in-cup deformity

🦋 Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) – ACR 1997 & SLICC 2012 Classification Criteria

ACR: ≥4 of 11 criteria; SLICC: ≥4 criteria (≥1 clinical + ≥1 immunologic) OR biopsy-proven LN + ANA/anti-dsDNA
ACR 1997 Criteria (requires 4 of 11, serially or simultaneously):
① Malar rash (fixed erythema, flat/raised, sparing nasolabial folds)
② Discoid rash (erythematous, raised patches with keratotic scaling, follicular plugging, scarring)
③ Photosensitivity (skin rash from UV exposure)
④ Oral ulcers (painless, oral or nasopharyngeal)
⑤ Arthritis (non-erosive, ≥2 peripheral joints)
⑥ Serositis (pleuritis – pleuritic pain, rub, effusion OR pericarditis – pain, rub, ECG, effusion)
⑦ Renal disorder (proteinuria >0.5 g/24h OR cellular casts – RBC, hemoglobin, granular, tubular, mixed)
⑧ Neurologic disorder (seizures or psychosis without other cause)
⑨ Hematologic disorder (hemolytic anemia OR leukopenia <4000 OR lymphopenia <1500 OR thrombocytopenia <100,000)
⑩ Immunologic disorder (anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, or antiphospholipid antibody – lupus anticoagulant, false-positive RPR, anticardiolipin)
⑪ Antinuclear antibody (ANA) – positive at any titer in absence of drugs known to cause drug-induced lupus
SLICC 2012 Criteria (higher sensitivity, requires ≥4 criteria with ≥1 clinical + ≥1 immunologic OR biopsy-proven LN + ANA/anti-dsDNA):
Clinical criteria (≥1): Acute cutaneous lupus, chronic cutaneous lupus, oral ulcers, alopecia (non-scarring), synovitis (≥2 joints) OR tenderness + morning stiffness ≥30 min, serositis, renal (proteinuria >0.5g/24h OR RBC casts), neurologic (seizures, psychosis, mononeuritis multiplex, myelitis, acute confusional state), hemolytic anemia, leukopenia (<4000) or lymphopenia (<1000), thrombocytopenia (<100,000)

✋ Systemic Sclerosis (SSc) – ACR/EULAR 2013 Classification Criteria

point-based (≥9 points) – major criterion (skin thickening proximal to MCPs) = 9 pts alone
ACR/EULAR 2013 Criteria – score additive (≥9 points = definite SSc):
• Skin thickening of fingers extending proximal to MCPs (9 pts) – sole criterion sufficient for diagnosis
• Finger swelling (puffy fingers, 2 pts) / Sclerodactyly (fingers only, 4 pts)
• Digital tip lesions (2 pts): pitting scars, ulceration, gangrene
• Telangiectasias (2 pts)
• Abnormal nailfold capillaries (2 pts): dilated, giant loops, dropout, avascular areas
• Pulmonary arterial hypertension (2 pts) or Interstitial lung disease (2 pts) – on echo/CT
• Raynaud phenomenon (2 pts)
• Scleroderma-specific autoantibodies (3 pts): anti-Scl-70 (topoisomerase I), anti-centromere, anti-RNA polymerase III

• Limited cutaneous SSc (lcSSc): skin thickening distal to elbows/knees, often anti-centromere, CREST syndrome (Calcinosis, Raynaud, Esophageal dysmotility, Sclerodactyly, Telangiectasias)
• Diffuse cutaneous SSc (dcSSc): proximal skin involvement (trunk), rapid progression, anti-Scl-70, high risk of renal crisis (scleroderma renal crisis – malignant hypertension, thrombotic microangiopathy)

💪 Polymyositis (PM) – Bohan & Peter 1975 Criteria / ENMC 2004

requires definite: 4/5 criteria (excluding rash); probable: 3/5
Bohan & Peter Criteria (1975) – for PM and Dermatomyositis (DM):
① Symmetric proximal muscle weakness (shoulder girdle, hip girdle, neck flexors) – progressive over weeks to months
② Elevated muscle enzymes (CK, aldolase, AST, ALT, LDH) – CK often >5× ULN in active PM
③ Myopathic changes on EMG (short duration, small amplitude MUAPs, spontaneous fibrillations, positive sharp waves, repetitive discharges)
④ Muscle biopsy abnormalities (necrosis, phagocytosis, regeneration, perifascicular atrophy – characteristic of DM, endomysial/perimysial inflammation with CD8+ T cells in PM)
⑤ Dermatomyositis-specific rash (heliotrope rash, Gottron papules, Gottron sign, shawl sign, mechanic's hands) – for DM (not present in PM)
ENMC 2004 Diagnostic Criteria for Idiopathic Inflammatory Myopathies (IIM):
• Definite PM: Progressive proximal weakness + elevated CK + myopathic EMG + muscle biopsy: endomysial CD8+ T cells invading non-necrotic muscle fibers (MHC-1 upregulation) + absence of rash, family history, dystrophinopathy, other myopathies
• Autoantibodies in PM: Anti-Jo-1 (antisynthetase syndrome – PM + interstitial lung disease + arthritis + Raynaud + mechanic's hands), Anti-SRP (severe, rapidly progressive), Anti-HMGCR (statin-associated necrotizing myopathy)

🫀 Takayasu Arteritis (Pulseless Disease) – ACR 1990 Classification Criteria

requires ≥3 of 6 criteria (sensitivity 90.5%, specificity 97.8%)
ACR 1990 Criteria for Takayasu Arteritis (≥3 of 6):
① Age at onset ≤40 years
② Claudication of extremities (limb ischemia with activity)
③ Decreased brachial artery pulse (unilateral or bilateral)
④ Difference in systolic BP between arms >10 mmHg
⑤ Bruit over subclavian arteries or aorta (audible on auscultation)
⑥ Arteriogram abnormality (stenosis, occlusion, dilation, aneurysm of aorta, primary branches, or large arteries) – not due to atherosclerosis, fibromuscular dysplasia
Imaging criteria (Numano classification based on vessel involvement):
• Type I: Aortic arch branches only
• Type IIa: Ascending aorta, arch, branches
• Type IIb: Type IIa + descending thoracic aorta
• Type III: Descending thoracic + abdominal aorta (classic middle aortic syndrome)
• Type IV: Abdominal aorta only (or renal arteries)
• Type V: Combined Type IIb + Type III (most common)

🩸 Polyarteritis Nodosa (PAN) – ACR 1990 Classification Criteria

requires ≥3 of 10 criteria (sensitivity 82.2%, specificity 86.6%)
ACR 1990 Criteria for Polyarteritis Nodosa (≥3 of 10):
① Weight loss >4 kg (unintentional)
② Livedo reticularis (mottled, net-like discoloration of skin)
③ Testicular pain or tenderness (not due to infection or trauma)
④ Mononeuropathy or polyneuropathy (mononeuritis multiplex most characteristic)
⑤ Myalgias (diffuse, not shoulder/hip girdle weakness), muscle tenderness, or leg muscle weakness
⑥ Diastolic BP >90 mmHg (hypertension – renal artery involvement)
⑦ Elevated BUN (>40 mg/dL) or Creatinine (>1.5 mg/dL) – not due to dehydration
⑨ Hepatitis B virus seropositivity (HBsAg or antibody) – present in 10-30% of PAN

• PAN: No glomerulonephritis (unlike MPA), no ANCA (usually), medium vessel predominant, HBV association common

🌬️ Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA – Churg-Strauss) – ACR 1990 Criteria

requires ≥4 of 6 criteria (sensitivity 85%, specificity 99.7%)

① Asthma (usually adult-onset, often severe, eosinophilic phenotype)
② Eosinophilia >10% on differential (>1500 cells/μL)
③ Mononeuropathy or polyneuropathy (mononeuritis multiplex – wrist drop, foot drop)
④ Migratory or transient pulmonary infiltrates on CXR (non-fixed)
⑤ Paranasal sinus abnormality (sinusitis, nasal polyps, allergic rhinitis)
⑥ Extravascular eosinophils on biopsy (artery, arteriole, venule – necrotizing vasculitis with eosinophilic infiltration)

• Age >65 years
• Cardiac manifestations (cardiomyopathy, heart failure, pericarditis)
• GI involvement (bleeding, perforation, pancreatitis)

🩸 IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein Purpura) – EULAR/PRINTO/PRES 2010 Criteria

requires palpable purpura (mandatory) + ≥1 of 4 criteria (pediatric) or skin biopsy showing IgA deposition (adults)
EULAR/PRINTO/PRES 2010 Classification Criteria for IgA Vasculitis (≥1 of):
Mandatory criterion: Palpable purpura (non-thrombocytopenic, often lower limb predominant) OR petechiae with lower limb predominance
Plus ≥1 of 4:
① Abdominal pain (acute onset, diffuse colicky, ± nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, GI bleeding)
② Histopathology (skin or kidney): leukocytoclastic vasculitis with predominant IgA deposition on immunofluorescence
③ Arthritis or arthralgia (acute onset, any joint)
④ Renal involvement (proteinuria >0.3 g/24h OR protein/creatinine ratio >30 mg/mmol OR hematuria >5 RBC/HPF or RBC casts)

• Renal involvement in 40-50% (adults more often progress to CKD compared to children)

🧬 Amyloidosis – Secondary (AA) Amyloidosis complicating Chronic Inflammatory Diseases

requires tissue biopsy with Congo red staining (apple-green birefringence) + immunohistochemistry or mass spectrometry typing
Types associated with rheumatic/autoimmune diseases:
• AA amyloidosis (secondary, reactive): Complication of chronic inflammation – RA (most common), ankylosing spondylitis, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, Still's disease, IBD, familial Mediterranean fever (FMF)
• AL amyloidosis (primary, plasma cell dyscrasia): Can mimic RA with joint involvement, carpal tunnel syndrome, periorbital purpura
• ATTR amyloidosis (transthyretin): Not primary rheumatic but can cause carpal tunnel, spinal stenosis
Diagnostic criteria for AA amyloidosis (requires ALL):
① Chronic inflammatory disease (duration >5-10 years, or persistently elevated acute phase reactants – CRP, SAA)
② Evidence of organ involvement (nephrotic syndrome – most common, hepatomegaly, restrictive cardiomyopathy, GI bleeding/malabsorption, peripheral neuropathy)
③ Tissue biopsy (abdominal fat pad aspirate: sensitivity 70-80%; rectal mucosa: 75-85%; kidney: >95% in renal involvement) with Congo red staining showing apple-green birefringence under polarized light
④ Immunohistochemistry or laser microdissection mass spectrometry confirming AA type (serum amyloid A protein)
Clinical clues to amyloidosis in rheumatic patients:
• Nephrotic-range proteinuria in patient with longstanding RA or AS (proteinuria >3.5 g/day, hypoalbuminemia, edema)
• Hepatomegaly with elevated alkaline phosphatase (not explained by primary disease)
• Restrictive cardiomyopathy (thickened LV walls, diastolic dysfunction, low voltage on ECG – pseudoinfarct pattern)
• Carpal tunnel syndrome (bilateral, infiltrative)
• Periorbital purpura (pinch purpura – after minor trauma, "raccoon eyes")
• Macroglossia (tongue enlargement with lateral indentations) – more common in AL but can occur in AA
• Orthostatic hypotension (autonomic neuropathy)
• Congo red stain of tissue: pathognomonic (apple-green birefringence)
Biopsy sites (order of preference in suspected systemic amyloidosis):
• Abdominal fat pad aspirate (least invasive, 80% sensitivity in AA/AL)
• Minor salivary gland (lip biopsy) (70-80% sensitivity)
• Rectal mucosa (75-85% sensitivity)
• Affected organ (kidney, liver, heart) – >95% sensitivity but invasive
SAA (Serum Amyloid A) level: elevated in active inflammation (goal of treatment: suppress SAA <10 mg/L to halt amyloid progression)

🦋 Thyrotoxicosis (Overt & Subclinical) – Diagnostic Criteria & Causes

TSH suppressed + FT4/T3 elevated (overt); TSH suppressed + FT4/T3 normal (subclinical)
Biochemical Diagnosis of Overt Thyrotoxicosis (requires ALL):
① TSH <0.4 mIU/L (often undetectable <0.01 mIU/L)
② Free T4 (FT4) >1.8 ng/dL (>23 pmol/L) OR Free T3 >4.2 pg/mL (>6.5 pmol/L)
③ T3 toxicosis: TSH suppressed + normal FT4 + elevated total T3 or FT3 (occurs in early Graves, toxic nodule)
Subclinical thyrotoxicosis: TSH <0.4 mIU/L + normal FT4 and FT3 (repeat in 2-4 weeks to confirm)
Etiologic Diagnosis of Thyrotoxicosis (based on RAIU, antibodies, ultrasound):
• Graves' disease: TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) positive (>95% sensitivity), TSI positive, diffuse increased uptake on RAIU (24h uptake >20-30%), ultrasound: diffuse heterogeneity, increased vascularity (thyroid inferno)
• Toxic multinodular goiter (Plummer disease): multiple nodules on exam/ultrasound, patchy increased uptake on RAIU, TRAb negative, older age
• Toxic adenoma (solitary hyperfunctioning nodule): single nodule, suppressed surrounding gland on RAIU (hot nodule), TRAb negative
• Subacute (De Quervain) thyroiditis: painful goiter, fever, elevated ESR/CRP, low RAIU (<5% at 24h), triphasic pattern (thyrotoxic → hypothyroid → recovery)
• Painless (silent) thyroiditis: no pain, low RAIU, often postpartum (postpartum thyroiditis), autoimmune (lymphocytic infiltration)
• Amiodarone-induced thyrotoxicosis (AIT): Type 1 (excess iodine-induced, low RAIU, may respond to thionamides), Type 2 (destructive thyroiditis, low RAIU, responds to steroids)

🦋 Hypothyroidism – Primary, Central, Subclinical

Primary: TSH >4.5 mIU/L + low FT4; Central: low/low-normal TSH + low FT4
Primary Overt Hypothyroidism (requires ALL):
① TSH elevated >4.5 mIU/L (typically >10 mIU/L in overt disease, often 20-100)
② Free T4 (FT4) decreased <0.8 ng/dL (<10 pmol/L)
③ Clinical symptoms: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, bradycardia, dry skin, hair loss, cognitive slowing, hoarse voice, delayed relaxation of DTRs (hung-up reflex)
Subclinical hypothyroidism: TSH 4.5-10 mIU/L with normal FT4 (treat if TSH >10, or if TSH 4.5-10 with positive TPO antibodies, pregnancy, symptoms, dyslipidemia, or infertility)
Etiologic Diagnosis:
• Hashimoto's thyroiditis (chronic autoimmune): TPOAb positive (>90%), TgAb positive (60%), ultrasound: heterogeneous echogenicity, hypoechoic micronodulation, reduced vascularity, may have goiter or atrophic gland
• Atrophic thyroiditis: TPOAb positive, small or non-palpable thyroid on ultrasound (end-stage fibrosis)
• Post-surgical/RAI hypothyroidism: history of thyroidectomy, radioactive iodine ablation for Graves, or neck irradiation
• Drug-induced: lithium, amiodarone, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, interferon-α, checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab)
• Central hypothyroidism: Low or inappropriately normal TSH with low FT4, often with other pituitary hormone deficiencies, pituitary/sellar mass on MRI
Myxedema Coma (life-threatening hypothyroidism) – requires ALL:
① Severe hypothyroidism (TSH markedly elevated, FT4 very low)
② Altered mental status (lethargy, stupor, coma)
③ Hypothermia (<35.5°C, often <30°C – may not be febrile even with infection)
④ Precipitating event: infection, cold exposure, sedation (opioids, benzodiazepines), MI, CVA, medications (amiodarone, lithium), discontinuation of levothyroxine
Other features: Bradycardia, hypotension (cardiogenic shock), hypoventilation (CO2 retention), hyponatremia (dilutional), hypoglycemia (adrenal insufficiency often coexists – check cortisol), pericardial/pleural effusion, ileus.
Treatment: IV levothyroxine (200-400 mcg loading then 1.6 mcg/kg/day) ± IV liothyronine (5-20 mcg q8h) if severe; IV hydrocortisone (100mg q8h) until adrenal insufficiency excluded; passive rewarming; ventilatory support; treat underlying precipitant

🩺 Autoimmune Thyroid Disease (AITD) – Graves' & Hashimoto's

Graves: TRAb positive + thyrotoxicosis ± ophthalmopathy; Hashimoto: TPOAb + TgAb + hypothyroidism or euthyroid with goiter
Graves' Disease (Diagnostic Triad – requires ≥2 of):
① Diffuse goiter (smooth, symmetric, ± bruit/thrill)
② Graves' ophthalmopathy (proptosis/exophthalmos, lid lag/retraction, periorbital edema, chemosis, diplopia, exposure keratopathy, optic neuropathy)
③ TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) positive (sensitivity 95-99%, specificity >99%) OR Thyroid stimulating immunoglobulin (TSI) positive
Supporting features: Thyroid acropachy (clubbing, periosteal new bone formation), pretibial myxedema (non-pitting, peau d'orange, erythematous plaques pretibial)
Hashimoto's Thyroiditis (Diagnostic Criteria – requires ≥2 of):
① Elevated TPOAb (anti-thyroid peroxidase antibodies) >34 IU/mL (positive in >90% of Hashimoto's)
② Elevated TgAb (anti-thyroglobulin antibodies) – positive in 60-80% (less specific)
③ Ultrasound findings: thyroid hypoechogenicity, heterogeneous parenchyma, micronodulation, pseudonodules, fibrous bands, reduced vascularity (late), possible goiter or atrophy
④ Lymphocytic infiltration on fine needle aspiration (rarely performed for diagnosis)
⑤ Patient may be euthyroid (compensated), subclinical hypothyroid (TSH elevated, normal FT4), or overt hypothyroid (TSH elevated, low FT4)

🦴 Primary Hyperparathyroidism (PHPT) – Diagnostic Criteria & Surgical Indications

hypercalcemia + elevated/inappropriately normal PTH + normal 25-OH vitamin D
Diagnosis of Primary Hyperparathyroidism (requires ALL):
① Hypercalcemia: total serum calcium >10.2 mg/dL (2.55 mmol/L) – confirmed on at least 2 occasions
② Elevated or inappropriately normal intact PTH >20 pg/mL (in setting of hypercalcemia, normal PTH = 10-65 pg/mL is inappropriate)
③ Normal or high 25-hydroxyvitamin D (>30 ng/mL) – vitamin D deficiency excluded or repleted (if deficient, replete and recheck)
④ Normal renal function or stable CKD (exclude secondary hyperparathyroidism – typically PTH elevated with normal/low calcium in CKD)
⑤ Exclusion of familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (FHH) if urinary calcium low – measure 24h urine calcium (FHH: <100 mg/24h, PHPT: usually >200 mg/24h)
Normocalcemic Primary Hyperparathyroidism (controversial diagnosis):
• Normal total and ionized calcium (confirmed multiple times)
• Elevated PTH (>65 pg/mL) on at least 2 occasions
• Normal 25-OH vitamin D (>30 ng/mL) – replete if deficient
• Normal eGFR (exclude secondary HPT from CKD)
• No secondary causes of elevated PTH (lithium, thiazides, malabsorption, renal calcium leak)
• Lower BMD (osteoporosis) or fragility fractures may be present
Surgical Indications for Parathyroidectomy (≥1 of following – 4th International Workshop):
• Age <50 years
• Serum calcium >1.0 mg/dL above upper limit of normal (>11.2 mg/dL in most labs)
• eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m²
• 24h urinary calcium >400 mg/day (nephrolithiasis risk)
• Kidney stones on imaging (any – even asymptomatic)
• Osteoporosis (T-score ≤ -2.5 at any site: lumbar spine, femoral neck, total hip, or 1/3 radius)
• Vertebral fracture on imaging (any, by VFA, DXA, X-ray, CT)
• Significant neurocognitive symptoms or myopathy interfering with daily activities

🦴 Hypoparathyroidism – Diagnosis & Etiology

hypocalcemia + low or inappropriately normal PTH + exclusion of other causes
Diagnosis of Hypoparathyroidism (requires ALL):
① Hypocalcemia: corrected total calcium <8.5 mg/dL (2.12 mmol/L) OR ionized calcium <1.12 mmol/L
② PTH low or inappropriately normal (PTH <20 pg/mL in setting of hypocalcemia)
③ Exclusion of other causes of hypocalcemia: severe vitamin D deficiency (25-OH vitamin D <20 ng/mL), CKD (eGFR <30), pancreatitis, hyperphosphatemia, medications (bisphosphonates, denosumab, cinacalcet, loop diuretics), magnesium deficiency (functional hypoparathyroidism – correct Mg first)
④ Normal or elevated serum phosphate (hyperphosphatemia) – due to lack of PTH-mediated phosphaturia
⑤ Normal or low 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol) – PTH normally stimulates 1-alpha-hydroxylase
Etiologies of Hypoparathyroidism:
• Post-surgical (most common – 75%): after total thyroidectomy, parathyroidectomy, radical neck dissection (transient or permanent; permanent if >6 months)
• Autoimmune (isolated or APS1): Autoimmune polyendocrine syndrome type 1 (APS1): hypoparathyroidism + adrenal insufficiency + chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis; anti-CaSR antibodies
• Genetic: DiGeorge syndrome (22q11.2 deletion), Barakat syndrome (HDR: hypoparathyroidism, deafness, renal disease), CASR mutation (activating), GCMB mutation
• Infiltrative: Hemochromatosis (iron overload), Wilson disease (copper), sarcoidosis, metastatic cancer
• Functional (reversible): Severe hypomagnesemia (<1.0 mg/dL) – impairs PTH secretion and action (correct magnesium first)
• Idiopathic (rare, often autoimmune)
Acute Hypocalcemia (requires emergent IV calcium if symptoms or QT prolongation):
• Symptoms: tetany (perioral numbness, carpopedal spasm – Trousseau sign, Chvostek sign), QT prolongation (risk of torsade de pointes), laryngospasm, seizures
• Treatment: IV calcium gluconate (1-2g over 10-20 min, then infusion 0.5-1.5 mg/kg/h) + calcitriol (0.5-1 mcg BID) + oral calcium
• Chronic treatment: elemental calcium 1-2g/day (calcium carbonate or citrate) + calcitriol 0.25-2 mcg/day; PTH replacement (rhPTH 1-84, Natpara) for refractory cases

💊 Primary Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome) – Endocrine Society Guidelines

ARR (Aldosterone-to-Renin Ratio) >20-30 + elevated aldosterone + suppressed renin + confirmatory testing
Screening (Case Detection) – requires ALL (off medications interfering with RAAS for 4 weeks):
① Aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR) >20 (using direct renin concentration, ng/L) or >30 (using PRA, ng/mL/h)
② Plasma aldosterone concentration (PAC) >15 ng/dL (415 pmol/L) – if PAC <10 ng/dL, hyperaldosteronism very unlikely
③ Plasma renin activity (PRA) suppressed (<1.0 ng/mL/h) OR direct renin concentration suppressed (<5 mIU/L)
Indications for screening: Hypertension + hypokalemia (spontaneous or diuretic-induced), resistant hypertension (uncontrolled on ≥3 medications), hypertension + adrenal incidentaloma, hypertension + OSA, hypertension + early onset (<40 years) or family history of hypertension/stroke, all patients with hypertension and atrial fibrillation (controversial)
Confirmatory Testing (≥1 abnormal = confirmed):
• Oral sodium loading test: high sodium diet (≥200 mmol/day) for 3 days – 24h urine aldosterone >12 μg/24h (33 nmol/day) on day 3
• Saline infusion test: IV 2L NS over 4 hours – post-infusion aldosterone >10 ng/dL (277 pmol/L) confirms
• Fludrocortisone suppression test (FST): aldosterone >6 ng/dL at day 4 (gold standard, cumbersome)
• Captopril challenge test: 1 hour post-captopril, aldosterone suppression <30% or aldosterone >15 ng/dL
Subtype Localization (adrenal CT and AVS):
• Adrenal CT (thin-cut, non-contrast): unilateral adenoma (aldosterone-producing adenoma – APA) vs bilateral hyperplasia (idiopathic hyperaldosteronism – IHA) vs unilateral hyperplasia (rare)
• Adrenal venous sampling (AVS) – gold standard: lateralization index (aldosterone ratio affected/contralateral) >4 (post-ACTH stimulation) or >2-3 (unstimulated) indicates unilateral source (surgery candidate). If AVS not feasible or failed, consider NP-59 scan or metomidate PET-CT
• Treatment: APA → unilateral laparoscopic adrenalectomy; IHA → mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (spironolactone 25-400mg/day or eplerenone 25-100mg BID)

🫀 Pheochromocytoma & Paraganglioma (PPGL) – Endocrine Society Guidelines

plasma metanephrines or urinary fractionated metanephrines >3× ULN; imaging; genetic testing
Biochemical Diagnosis (gold standard – plasma free metanephrines or urinary fractionated metanephrines):
• Plasma free metanephrines (supine after 30 min rest): Normetanephrine >0.9 nmol/L (170 pg/mL) OR Metanephrine >0.5 nmol/L (100 pg/mL) – sensitivity 97-99%, specificity 85-95%. >3× ULN → diagnostic
• Urinary fractionated metanephrines (24h): Normetanephrine >900 mcg/24h OR Metanephrine >400 mcg/24h
• Plasma or urinary catecholamines (less sensitive, not first-line): norepinephrine, epinephrine, dopamine
• Chromogranin A (supportive): elevated in most PPGL, but non-specific (also elevated in renal failure, PPI use, neuroendocrine tumors)
Interpretation & Pre-test Preparation:
• Drugs to avoid before testing (withdraw under supervision): tricyclic antidepressants, SNRIs, levodopa, buspirone, MAOIs, acetaminophen (interferes with some assays), alpha- and beta-blockers (may require adjustment)
• False positives: Uncontrolled hypertension, acute stress, illness, sleep apnea, caffeine, smoking, alcohol, decongestants (pseudoephedrine)
• If mild elevation (1-3× ULN) without symptoms: repeat testing after optimizing conditions, consider clonidine suppression test (normetanephrine fails to suppress >50% suggests true PPGL)
Imaging Localization (after biochemical confirmation):
• CT adrenal/abdomen/pelvis (first-line): adrenal mass (pheochromocytoma: >10 HU non-contrast, heterogeneous, necrotic, cystic) or extra-adrenal paraganglioma (along sympathetic chain – organ of Zuckerkandl, carotid body, jugulotympanic)
• MRI (if CT contraindicated, or metastatic risk): T2 "light bulb" bright signal characteristic
• Functional imaging (if metastatic, extra-adrenal, recurrent, genetic syndrome): 68Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT (highest sensitivity), 123I-MIBG (previously first-line), 18F-FDG PET/CT (especially in SDHx mutations)
• Genetic testing (all PPGL patients): SDHB, SDHD, SDHA, SDHC, VHL, RET (MEN2), NF1, MAX, TMEM127 (up to 40% heritable)
Preoperative Preparation (requires alpha-blockade BEFORE beta-blockade):
• Start alpha-blockade 7-14 days pre-op: Phenoxybenzamine (10mg BID, titrate up to 1mg/kg/day) OR selective alpha-1 blockers (doxazosin, prazosin)
• Add beta-blocker only after adequate alpha-blockade (to prevent unopposed alpha-mediated hypertensive crisis): propranolol, atenolol, metoprolol
• High-sodium diet (to restore volume after alpha-blockade)
• IV fluids (normal saline) day before surgery
• Intraoperative management: Remifentanil, nitroprusside, phentolamine for hypertensive crisis

🦷 Acromegaly – Diagnostic Criteria (Endocrine Society 2014)

IGF-1 elevated + GH nadir >1 mcg/L after OGTT (or >0.4 mcg/L with high-sensitivity assay)
Biochemical Diagnosis (requires BOTH):
① Elevated age- and sex-adjusted IGF-1 (most sensitive and specific, single measurement – first-line screening) – > upper limit of normal for age/sex
② Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) with 75g glucose: GH nadir >1 mcg/L (1 ng/mL) using older assays, OR >0.4 mcg/L (0.4 ng/mL) using high-sensitivity assays – gold standard (failure to suppress GH after glucose load)
Note: Random GH is not diagnostic (pulsatile secretion, but GH >10 mcg/L in symptomatic patient highly suggestive)
Clinical Features (supporting diagnosis – acral and facial changes):
• Acral enlargement: enlarged hands (ring size increase), feet (shoe size increase), frontal bossing, prognathism (protruding jaw), macroglossia, thickened calvarium
• Soft tissue changes: carpal tunnel syndrome (50%), coarse facial features, oily skin, hyperhidrosis, skin tags, acanthosis nigricans
• Arthropathy: degenerative arthritis, joint pain (wrists, knees, hips, spine)
• Cardiovascular: hypertension (30-50%), cardiomyopathy (concentric LVH, diastolic dysfunction), arrhythmias
• Metabolic: diabetes mellitus or impaired glucose tolerance (20-50%), hypertriglyceridemia
• Sleep apnea (50-80%), colonic polyps (increased colon cancer risk), goiter, hypogonadism, hyperprolactinemia (due to stalk compression or co-secretion)
Localization & Treatment:
• MRI pituitary (3T with contrast): macroadenoma (>1 cm, 60-70%) or microadenoma (<1 cm, 30%) – assess chiasmal compression, cavernous sinus invasion (Knosp score)
• Treatment: Transsphenoidal surgery (first-line for microadenoma and macroadenoma without cavernous invasion) → medical therapy (somatostatin analogs: octreotide, lanreotide; GH receptor antagonist: pegvisomant; dopamine agonist: cabergoline) → radiation (stereotactic radiotherapy) for residual disease
• Cure criteria post-surgery: Normal age-adjusted IGF-1, GH nadir <0.4-1 mcg/L after OGTT, random GH <1 mcg/L

🧠 Hypopituitarism (Panhypopituitarism) – Diagnostic Criteria & Testing

deficiency of ≥1 pituitary hormones (central hypothyroidism, hypogonadism, ACTH deficiency, GH deficiency)
Diagnosis requires deficiency in at least one axis (often multiple) with evidence of pituitary/hypothalamic disease:
Central Adrenal Insufficiency (ACTH deficiency) – most dangerous:
• Morning serum cortisol <3 mcg/dL (<80 nmol/L) with low or inappropriately normal ACTH – suggests deficiency. Cortisol 3-15 mcg/dL → requires stimulation testing (cosyntropin 250mcg IV – peak cortisol <18 mcg/dL at 30-60 min suggests deficiency; but after 2 weeks of ACTH deficiency, cosyntropin test may be falsely normal; insulin tolerance test or metyrapone test is gold standard)
• Symptoms: fatigue, hypotension, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia, nausea, weight loss (no hyperpigmentation – unlike primary adrenal insufficiency)
Central Hypothyroidism (TSH deficiency):
• Low or inappropriately normal TSH (0.4-4.5 mIU/L but often <2) with low free T4 (<0.8 ng/dL) – hallmark (TSH should be elevated with low T4 in primary thyroid disease)
• Clinical: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, bradycardia, cognitive slowing (same as primary hypothyroidism but no goiter)
• Treatment: Levothyroxine (target FT4 mid-upper normal, not TSH – TSH cannot be used to monitor)
Central Hypogonadism (FSH/LH deficiency):
• Low or inappropriately normal FSH/LH with low sex steroids: low testosterone (males, <250 ng/dL), low estradiol (females, <20 pg/mL) with oligomenorrhea or amenorrhea
• In premenopausal women, distinguishes from primary ovarian insufficiency (where FSH/LH are elevated)
• Prolactin may be elevated (stalk compression – disconnection hyperprolactinemia) or low
• Treatment: Sex hormone replacement (testosterone, estrogen/progesterone) unless fertility desired (then gonadotropin therapy)
Growth Hormone Deficiency (adult – requires stimulation testing):
• IGF-1 low (age/sex-adjusted) – supportive but normal IGF-1 does not exclude GHD (30% of adults with GHD have normal IGF-1)
• Stimulation testing: Insulin tolerance test (ITT – gold standard, peak GH <3-5 mcg/L), GHRH+arginine, glucagon stimulation test, or macimorelin
• Clinical: increased body fat (visceral), reduced lean mass, fatigue, reduced exercise capacity, dyslipidemia, reduced bone density
• Treatment: Recombinant human GH (0.1-0.3 mg/day, titrated by IGF-1 and clinical response)
Causes of Hypopituitarism (MRI pituitary required):
• Pituitary adenoma (most common – 60%): macroadenoma >1 cm → mass effect (visual field defects – bitemporal hemianopsia, headache, hypopituitarism)
• Pituitary apoplexy (sudden hemorrhage into adenoma): sudden headache, vision loss, ophthalmoplegia, adrenal crisis (emergent hydrocortisone before imaging)
• Sheehan syndrome (postpartum pituitary necrosis): lactation failure, amenorrhea, fatigue after delivery
• Craniopharyngioma (suprasellar, children/young adults): calcified cystic mass, vision loss, hypopituitarism, diabetes insipidus
• Empty sella syndrome (primary or secondary): partially or completely empty sella turcica on MRI, variable hypopituitarism
• Infiltrative: Sarcoidosis (hypothalamic involvement, diabetes insipidus), Langerhans cell histiocytosis, hemochromatosis
• Traumatic brain injury (TBI), pituitary surgery or radiation, lymphocytic hypophysitis (autoimmune, peripartum)

🩸 Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (T1DM) – Diagnostic Criteria & Autoimmune Markers

hyperglycemia (ADA criteria) + autoimmune markers (≥1 autoantibody) + low C-peptide
Diagnosis of Type 1 Diabetes (requires ALL for classic presentation, or hyperglycemia criteria + autoantibodies):
① Hyperglycemia meeting ADA criteria (FPG ≥126 mg/dL, 2h OGTT ≥200 mg/dL, HbA1c ≥6.5%, or random glucose ≥200 mg/dL with classic symptoms)
② Presence of ≥1 islet autoantibody (GAD65, IA-2, ZnT8, ICA, insulin autoantibody - IAA if age <10)
③ Low or absent C-peptide (fasting <0.2 nmol/L, or post-meal/glucagon <0.6 nmol/L) – indicates absolute insulin deficiency
④ Age typically <30 years (but can occur at any age – LADA in adults)
⑤ No evidence of type 2 diabetes (not obese, no acanthosis nigricans, no metabolic syndrome, negative family history T2DM in most)
Autoantibody Patterns in T1DM:
• GAD65 (glutamic acid decarboxylase): most common in adults (LADA – latent autoimmune diabetes in adults), positive in 70-80% at diagnosis
• IA-2 (insulinoma-associated protein 2): more common in children, positive in 50-70%
• ZnT8 (zinc transporter 8): increases sensitivity (adds 10-15% when GAD65 and IA-2 negative)
• ICA (islet cell cytoplasmic antibodies): traditional but less used now
• IAA (insulin autoantibodies): positive in young children (<10 years), before exogenous insulin exposure
Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults (LADA) – criteria (≥1 of):
① Adult onset (>30 years) with initial diagnosis of type 2 diabetes but not obese, no metabolic syndrome, or poor glycemic control on oral agents
② Positive GAD65 antibody (most common) or other islet autoantibody
③ Low C-peptide (often initially normal but declines faster than T2DM)
④ Younger age at diagnosis (<50 years) than typical T2DM, often normal BMI or lean
Note: LADA progresses to insulin dependence faster than T2DM (months to a few years)

📉 Hypoglycemia in Diabetes – ADA Classification & Whipple's Triad

Whipple's triad: symptoms + low glucose + resolution with glucose administration
Whipple's Triad (diagnostic gold standard – requires ALL 3):
① Symptoms and/or signs consistent with hypoglycemia (autonomic: sweating, palpitations, tremor, hunger, anxiety; neuroglycopenic: confusion, dizziness, weakness, visual changes, seizure, coma)
② Documented low plasma glucose: <70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L) – alert value; clinically significant <54 mg/dL (3.0 mmol/L)
③ Resolution of symptoms after glucose administration (oral or IV)
ADA Hypoglycemia Severity Classification (for patients with diabetes):
• Level 1 (Alert value): Glucose <70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L) – patient aware, may self-treat
• Level 2 (Clinically significant): Glucose <54 mg/dL (3.0 mmol/L) – requires attention, cognitive function may be impaired
• Level 3 (Severe): Severe cognitive impairment requiring assistance of another person – regardless of glucose level (may be unconscious, seizure, unable to self-treat)
Causes of Hypoglycemia in Diabetic Patients (consider if recurrent):
• Excess insulin or sulfonylureas: dosing error, missed meals, increased activity, renal impairment (decreased clearance of insulin or sulfonylureas)
• Counter-regulatory failure (hypoglycemia unawareness): autonomic neuropathy, recurrent hypoglycemia (defective glucose counter-regulation, impaired epinephrine response)
• Alcohol intake (impairs gluconeogenesis), hepatic failure, severe heart failure (cardiac cachexia), chronic kidney disease (eGFR <30)
• Insulinoma (rare, but consider if non-diabetic or severe recurrent hypoglycemia despite insulin reduction)
Treatment of Hypoglycemia ("Rule of 15"):
• Conscious (able to swallow): 15g rapid-acting carbohydrate (4 oz juice or regular soda, 3-4 glucose tablets, 1 tbsp sugar or honey, 5-6 hard candies). Recheck glucose in 15 min. If still <70 mg/dL, repeat 15g. Once >70 mg/dL, eat a meal or snack with protein (to prevent rebound)
• Unconscious or unable to swallow: Glucagon (1 mg IM or subcut, or 2 mg intranasal) OR IV dextrose (25-50 mL of D50W over 1-3 min, or 1 mg/kg of 10% dextrose infusion). If no response after 10-15 min, repeat glucagon or dextrose
• After severe hypoglycemia, monitor glucose for 12-24h (risk of recurrent hypoglycemia due to persistent insulin effect or long-acting sulfonylureas). Octreotide for sulfonylurea-induced hypoglycemia (50-100 mcg subcut q6h) may be required if refractory to dextrose

🩸 Ulcerative Colitis (UC) – Montreal Classification, Mayo Score, Endoscopic Severity

Montreal: E1 (proctitis), E2 (left-sided), E3 (extensive/pancolitis); Mayo score ≥2 for active disease

Montreal: L1 (ileal), L2 (colonic), L3 (ileocolonic), L4 (upper GI); B1 (non-stricturing non-penetrating), B2 (stricturing), B3 (penetrating), p (perianal modifier)

• Abdominal pain (most common – 70-80%): right lower quadrant or periumbilical (terminal ileal involvement), colicky, postprandial, may precede obstruction

🔥 Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) – Montreal Definition, LA Classification, Management

Montreal: troublesome symptoms (heartburn/regurgitation ≥2x/week) or complications from reflux; Los Angeles (LA) grade A-D for erosive esophagitis
Clinical Presentation (Typical and Atypical Symptoms):
• Typical (esophageal) symptoms: Heartburn (retrosternal burning, worse after meals, lying down, bending forward; relieved by antacids), Regurgitation (effortless return of acidic or bilious fluid into mouth, worse supine), Dysphagia (20-30% – suggests stricture or esophageal dysmotility), Odynophagia (painful swallowing – alarm symptom, suggests severe esophagitis, ulcer, or infection – CMV, herpes, candida), Globus (lump in throat sensation)

🟡 Choledocholithiasis (CBD Stone) – ERCP Indications, Clinical Prediction Rules (ASGE Criteria, Tokyo Guidelines)

Clinical Presentation of Choledocholithiasis (spectrum from asymptomatic to cholangitis):

🩸 Portal Hypertension – HVPG Measurement, Causes (Prehepatic, Hepatic, Posthepatic), Baveno Criteria

Clinically significant portal hypertension: HVPG ≥10 mmHg (esophageal varices develop); HVPG ≥12 mmHg: risk of variceal hemorrhage
Hepatic Venous Pressure Gradient (HVPG – gold standard for diagnosis and prognosis):
HVPG = wedged hepatic venous pressure (WHVP) – free hepatic venous pressure (FHVP). Normal: 1-5 mmHg. Portal hypertension (PH): HVPG 6-9 mmHg (subclinical), >10 mmHg: clinically significant portal hypertension (CSPH – develops varices, risk of decompensation). >12 mmHg: risk of variceal hemorrhage increases exponentially. >16 mmHg: high risk of mortality, hepatic decompensation.

⚠️ Acute Liver Failure (ALF) – Definition, Etiologies, King's College Criteria, Clichy Criteria

Coagulopathy (INR ≥1.5) + encephalopathy within 26 weeks of jaundice in patient without preexisting liver disease
AASLD Definition of Acute Liver Failure (ALF):
① Evidence of acute liver injury (severe elevation of aminotransferases – AST/ALT often >10,000 U/L in ischemic/toxic injury, or lower in viral hepatitis (500-3000 U/L))
② Coagulopathy: INR ≥1.5 (or prothrombin time >15 seconds) – cannot be corrected with vitamin K
③ Hepatic encephalopathy (any grade – grade I-IV) – hallmark distinguishing ALF from acute hepatitis (ALF has encephalopathy, acute hepatitis does not)
④ Onset of encephalopathy within 26 weeks (usually 8 weeks) of first symptoms (jaundice, malaise, anorexia) in patient without known chronic liver disease
Hyperacute liver failure (onset <7 days): Most severe – often due to acetaminophen toxicity, ischemia (shock liver), Amanita phalloides mushroom poisoning. High incidence of cerebral edema, but highest spontaneous survival (60-70% with transplant-free survival in acetaminophen).
Acute liver failure (onset 8-28 days): Viral hepatitis (A, B, E, EBV, CMV), drug-induced liver injury (DILI – anti-TB, NSAIDs, antiepileptics, herbal supplements), autoimmune hepatitis, Wilson disease.
Subacute liver failure (onset 5-26 weeks): Indolent presentation with ascites, jaundice, encephalopathy later. Worse prognosis (transplant-free survival 20-30%), often due to idiosyncratic DILI, seronegative hepatitis, indeterminate cause.
King's College Criteria (most widely used – predicts need for liver transplantation, poor prognosis):
Acetaminophen (paracetamol) toxicity (ALF due to APAP):
① pH <7.3 (24h after ingestion) OR
② All of: PT >100 seconds (INR >6.5) + Creatinine >3.4 mg/dL (300 μmol/L) + Grade III/IV encephalopathy
Non-acetaminophen ALF (viral, drug-induced, autoimmune, Wilson, Budd-Chiari, indeterminate):
① INR >6.5 (PT >100 seconds) OR
② Any 3 of: Age <10 years or >40 years + Etiology: non-A non-B hepatitis, halothane, idiosyncratic DILI + Jaundice to encephalopathy interval >7 days + Bilirubin >18 mg/dL (300 μmol/L) + PT >50 seconds (INR >3.5)
Clichy Criteria (French – alternative, used in Europe for viral hepatitis): Factor V level <20% (<30% if age <30 years) + encephalopathy grade III/IV. Factor V <30% has 90% positive predictive value for death without transplant.
Management of Acute Liver Failure (ICU admission, multidisciplinary: hepatology, critical care, transplant surgery):
• N-acetylcysteine (NAC) – for all ALF (especially acetaminophen, but beneficial in non-APAP as well): APAP protocol: 150 mg/kg IV over 15-60 min (loading dose), then 50 mg/kg over 4h, then 100 mg/kg over 16h. Non-APAP: 150 mg/kg over 15 min, then 12.5 mg/kg/h for 24-72h (improves transplant-free survival in early stage, grades I-II encephalopathy).
• Neurologic management (cerebral edema – leading cause of death in ALF): Head CT (rule out intracranial hemorrhage), mannitol (0.5-1 g/kg IV bolus for acute elevation ICP, if serum osmolality <320 mOsm/kg), hyperventilation (transient – decrease PaCO2 to 25-30 mmHg, only for herniation crisis), therapeutic hypothermia (33-34°C) (controversial, monitor for arrhythmias, coagulopathy), ICP monitor (invasive – EVD) in selected patients (if transplant candidate, grade IV encephalopathy). Treat seizures (phenytoin prophylaxis not routine, treat if clinical/EEG seizures).
• Hemodynamics and renal support: Maintain MAP >65 mmHg (vasopressors: norepinephrine, vasopressin). Avoid hypovolemia, but also avoid overhydration (increased ICP). Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) for AKI (avoid intermittent HD – fluid shifts increase ICP).
• Coagulopathy (INR elevated but rebleeding rare unless active bleeding): Do NOT correct INR with FFP or platelets unless active bleeding or invasive procedure (transplant surgery, ICP monitor placement). FFP increases intravascular volume, may worsen cerebral edema. Vitamin K 10 mg SQ/IV (rule out deficiency).
• Metabolic management: Maintain glucose 80-120 mg/dL (insulin infusion if hyperglycemic, D10/D20 if hypoglycemic). Hypophosphatemia common after acetaminophen (repletion). Consider ammonia-lowering therapy (lactulose, rifaximin, CRRT for severe hyperammonemia >150-200 μmol/L).
• Liver transplantation (the only definitive therapy for severe ALF meeting King's College criteria or progressive deterioration): Urgent evaluation for transplantation. Contraindications: irreversible brain injury (herniation, absent brainstem reflexes), uncontrolled sepsis, multi-organ failure with >3 organ systems, advanced malignancy. Living donor liver transplantation possible in selected centers for ALF (right lobe graft). Auxiliary partial orthotopic liver transplantation (APOLT) for potential native liver regeneration (reserve native liver, implant partial graft – if native recovers, immunosuppression weaned).
• Investigational/Bridge therapies: High-volume plasmapheresis (removes toxins, cytokines, ammonia – improves survival in some studies), MARS (Molecular Adsorbent Recirculating System – albumin dialysis), Prometheus (fractionated plasma separation and adsorption), bioartificial liver (rare, not standard).

Clinical presentation: Hematemesis (bright red blood or "coffee grounds"), melena, hematochezia (severe bleed), hypovolemic shock (tachycardia, hypotension, orthostasis, syncope). Precipitants: bacterial infection (SBP – 30-40% of bleeds associated with infection), portal vein thrombosis, increased portal pressure (after heavy meal, straining, vomiting), NSAID use.
Endoscopic stigmata predicting rebleeding (presence requires secondary prophylaxis intensification): Active spurting (Forrest Ia – rebleed 90% without therapy), Active oozing (Forrest Ib – rebleed 10-20%), Non-bleeding visible vessel (Forrest IIa – rebleed 40-50%), Adherent clot (Forrest IIb – rebleed 20-30%), Flat pigmented spot (Forrest IIc – 5-10%), Clean base (Forrest III – <5%).

Primary Prophylaxis of Variceal Hemorrhage (prevent first bleed – indicated for medium/large varices or small varices with red wale signs or Child C):
• Non-selective beta-blockers (NSBB): Propranolol (starting 20 mg BID, titrate to heart rate 55-60 bpm, maximum 320 mg/day) OR Nadolol (starting 40 mg daily, titrate to heart rate 55-60 bpm, maximum 240 mg/day). Carvedilol (6.25-12.5 mg daily) – superior HVPG reduction but more hypotension risk.
• Endoscopic variceal ligation (EVL) – alternative to NSBB if contraindications (asthma, heart block, hypotension, decompensated cirrhosis). Equally effective to NSBB.
Secondary Prophylaxis (prevent rebleeding – after acute bleed): NSBB (propranolol/nadolol/carvedilol) + EVL repeated q2-4 weeks until variceal obliteration (NSBB continued indefinitely). TIPS for failure of combination therapy (recurrent bleeding on NSBB+EVL).

🩺 Malabsorption Syndrome – Causes (Pancreatic, Biliary, Mucosal, Lymphatic), Diagnostic Workup (Fecal Fat, D-xylose, Breath Tests, Small Bowel Biopsy)

Quantitative fecal fat >7 g/day (or Sudan stain positive); qualitative testing for specific deficiencies
Clinical Manifestations of Malabsorption (nutrient-specific deficiencies):
• General symptoms: Chronic diarrhea (watery, bulky, greasy, foul-smelling – steatorrhea), weight loss (despite normal or increased appetite), fatigue, weakness, bloating, flatulence, abdominal distension, failure to thrive (children), pallor, edema (hypoalbuminemia), easy bruising (vitamin K deficiency).
• Fat malabsorption (steatorrhea): Pale, bulky, greasy, foul-smelling stools that float (high fat content), adhere to toilet bowl. Causes: pancreatic insufficiency (most common – chronic pancreatitis, cystic fibrosis), bacterial overgrowth (deconjugated bile acids), celiac disease (mucosal injury), short bowel syndrome, lymphangiectasia.
• Vitamin deficiencies (specific syndromes): Vitamin A (night blindness, xerophthalmia, Bitot spots), Vitamin D (osteomalacia, bone pain, fractures, hypocalcemia, secondary hyperparathyroidism), Vitamin E (peripheral neuropathy, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia, retinopathy), Vitamin K (ecchymoses, bleeding, elevated INR).
• Mineral deficiencies: Iron (microcytic anemia, koilonychia, pica), Calcium (tetany, paresthesias, Chvostek/Trousseau signs, osteopenia/osteoporosis), Magnesium (neuromuscular irritability, arrhythmias, hypocalcemia refractory to calcium), Zinc (alopecia, dermatitis (acral/periorificial), impaired wound healing, hypogeusia, diarrhea).
• Carbohydrate malabsorption (lactose, fructose, sorbitol): Bloating, flatulence, watery diarrhea, abdominal pain after ingestion. Diagnose with hydrogen breath test (rise >20 ppm within 90-180 min after lactulose/fructose).
• Protein malabsorption: Edema (hypoalbuminemia), muscle wasting, kwashiorkor (pediatrics – ascites, dermatosis, fatty liver).
• B12 deficiency (terminal ileum – Crohn's, resection, bacterial overgrowth, pernicious anemia): Macrocytic anemia, subacute combined degeneration (paresthesias, ataxia, impaired vibration/proprioception), glossitis.

Treatment of Malabsorption (based on underlying cause):
• Pancreatic insufficiency (EPI): Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) – lipase 40,000-80,000 units with meals (500-1000 U/kg/meal). Take with first bite, not at end. Fat-soluble vitamins (ADEK) supplementation.
• Celiac disease: Lifelong strict gluten-free diet (avoid wheat, barley, rye, cross-contaminated oats). Nutritional deficiencies repletion (iron, folate, B12, D, calcium, zinc). Monitoring: annual tTG-IgA titers (should normalize on diet), bone density, nutritional panels.
• Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO): Rifaximin 550 mg TID x 14 days (if hydrogen predominant). Add neomycin 500 mg BID x 10 days if methane predominant (or rifaximin + metronidazole). Treat underlying cause (prokinetics for dysmotility, PPI withdrawal, surgical strictureplasty).
• Bile acid malabsorption (ileal resection, Crohn's ileitis, idiopathic): Bile acid sequestrants: cholestyramine 4g QD-BID before meals (reduce diarrhea). Avoid in high-output ostomy (worsens dehydration).
• Short bowel syndrome (massive small bowel resection): Teduglutide (GLP-2 analog, 0.05 mg/kg daily SC) – improves intestinal adaptation, reduces parenteral nutrition dependence. Imodium, loperamide, codeine for symptom control. Low-fat, high-protein, high-calorie diet. • Intestinal lymphangiectasia (protein-losing enteropathy): Low-fat diet (replace with MCT oil – medium-chain triglycerides absorbed directly, not via lymphatics), octreotide (reduces lymph flow). Treat underlying cause if secondary (Whipple, lymphoma, sarcoidosis, constrictive pericarditis).
• Tropical sprue (after travel to endemic areas – Caribbean, India, Southeast Asia, Africa): Tetracycline 250-500 mg QID or doxycycline 100 mg BID + folic acid 5 mg daily for 3-6 months. Most respond, relapse may occur.
